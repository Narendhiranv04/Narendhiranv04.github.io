Contracts as the spine of long-horizon manipulation

IIIT Hyderabad · Visual HRL with SmolVLA & Mixture-of-Experts

I am wrapping every option in our hierarchical controller with temporal contracts so the arm must prove pre- and post-conditions before it acts. The system stitches a SmolVLA encoder, a Mixture-of-Experts option selector, and a symbolic safety monitor into one dependable loop.

Why contracts. Long-horizon policies crumble when a sub-skill drifts outside its safe set. Encoding LTL-style contracts around each option forces the controller to certify that prerequisites hold and that post-conditions are achieved before handing control back to the planner.

Stack. A SmolVLA encoder supplies visual tokens, a Mixture-of-Experts actor scores options, and a symbolic monitor vetoes unsafe transitions. When the MoE hesitates or violates timing bounds, a low-latency MPC fallback steps in to keep the arm moving.

Breakthrough. The “contract buffer” made the architecture usable. It carries risk scores and satisfaction margins between stages so the planner can stay adventurous without tripping the monitor. Latency stayed inside budget and we finally had a dial for trading off caution versus exploration.

System view. Simulation traces stream into dashboards for trajectory slack, LTL violations, and MoE gating entropy. Watching those signals live highlights the moment an option begins hallucinating or when perception noise knocks an invariant off the rails.

Current benchmarks. We are midway through cluttered-shelf manipulation suites. The monitor is holding up under occlusions, but we are still tuning how aggressively the MoE explores when perception confidence dips.

Next steps. The next milestone is a hybrid rollout on a Franka arm inside a reset-free tabletop scene. The contracts need to hold at 20 Hz without inflating latency, so we are profiling the monitor’s C++ bindings and pruning redundant checks.

Reliability is the feature—not the afterthought.
