Assistive Robotics Intern · Jan 2025 – May 2025

Torque prediction that keeps the wearer in the loop

Monash University · Sit-to-walk exoskeleton controller

I distilled a gated recurrent network to 68k parameters and paired it with a fuzzy supervisor so our sit-to-walk exoskeleton could deliver torque inside 20 ms. The work centred on feeling invisible to the wearer while staying accurate under noisy IMU and EMG signals.

Challenge. Sit-to-walk transitions punish latency. If the assistive torque lags by more than a few milliseconds the wearer feels a drag. We needed a controller that predicted joint torques almost instantaneously while handling variable gait phases.

Approach. I pruned the feature set to the most informative IMU and EMG channels, built a compact GRU, and wrapped it with a fuzzy supervisor that modulates assistance based on gait-phase confidence. The result was a model small enough to live on an embedded Jetson.

Iterating. Quantisation plus tensor core inference were key to hitting our timing budget. With mixed-precision kernels we kept inference under 20 ms without sacrificing fidelity, and the controller stopped feeling like it was chasing the user.

What we learned. The fuzzy layer smoothed out torque spikes caused by imperfect foot contact, dropping peak error by 18%. Test subjects stopped noticing the controller catching up—the assistance felt native rather than corrective.

Reflection. Assistive robotics is equal parts empathy and engineering. Every watt has to arrive in sync with the wearer’s intent, and the controller should disappear into the motion.

Lightweight models can still feel heavy—unless you design for the human first.
